# Intro

I started with opcodes.

You know — programming a computer with hex codes on a hex keyboard. Like playing a game on hard mode: one mistake, and you start again.

Then I discovered mnemonics.

Oh god. What a joy. Zilog. I was in love.

Imagine it: no more opcodes, no more memorising tables. It’s all right there. You assemble it 1:1 and the machine does exactly what you told it to do.

Perfection.

Then C showed up and I thought: who needs this bloated, inefficient thing?

Well — I did.

I’d set up a main() as a prelude, open an asm {} section, and just… code. It was worth it for .exe. (Not .com. That thing had to be assembly.) C was too fat and too slow to fit on a page between two IRQs.

Yeah. C sucked.

Until it didn’t.

And slowly, over the years, a lifetime love affair began.

The others came and went, flexing their muscles.
Let’s add classes to C. Let’s add streaming models. Let’s add frameworks and paradigms and abstractions and—

Yeah, right. I was way too stupid for that.

Time passed. Objective-C, C#, and whatnot. Cool languages. Interesting. Powerful. Sometimes even fun.

Still…

C was for me.
C was enough.

And then I got old.

And so did C.

One day I decided I should try something different. After a decade of failure — garbage collectors, reference counting, cleverness layered on cleverness — I gave up.

All I wanted was C, wearing nicer clothes.

Not better. Not cooler. Not smarter.

I didn’t want Better C. I wanted a grown-up C. A C for people who’ve been there for decades, trying to debug pointer-to-pointer-to-pointer arguments passed “by reference.”

Yeah.

That’s why assembly is cool: no lies.

And here we are.

Lumen.

I saw the light.

This book is the book of that light.

A book about C with hindsight.

Illuminating.
